# "Proving Nothing" — The Builder's Edition (Outline Draft: Systems / Practitioner Lens)

*Architect lens: the practitioner who wants to **build and ship** ZK systems. Organize so the real stack and the real decisions come first; theory is admitted exactly where a builder must understand it to choose correctly. Keeps the seven-layer trust-decomposition thesis and the Feynman rule (intuition/analogy hook, then the engineering detail).*

---

## 1. One-paragraph thesis

A zero-knowledge system is not a proof — it is a **stack of engineering decisions**, each of which can be wrong, slow, or insecure independently of the others. This edition organizes the book as the **decision tree a builder actually walks**: pick a language, generate a witness on real hardware, choose an arithmetization, choose a proving system and its polynomial commitment, decide whether to recurse or fold for scale, package it as a zkVM or a hand-rolled circuit, and finally pay gas to a verifier contract that a committee can still rug. The seven layers are retained because they *are* the decision points — each layer is "a thing you choose, a cost you pay, and a way you get breached." Theory (sum-check, IOPs, GKR, MLE, QAP, pairings, the AGM) is not a separate "foundations" wing; it is folded into the layer where the builder's hand is forced by it — you learn *why multilinear* the moment you must choose a commitment scheme, you learn the *AGM* the moment you must defend a Groth16 deployment. The spine is **"when to use what, what it costs, how it fails."** Every chapter opens with a shipping decision and closes with a cost model and a failure mode. The reader finishes able to architect a production ZK system and defend each of its seven trust assumptions in a design review.

## 2. The full outline

**Five parts, 22 chapters.** The arc follows the build pipeline: *Decide → Encode → Prove → Scale → Ship*. The seven layers are the connective tissue; Parts II–IV walk them in build order, not numeric order.

### PART I — THE DECISION (why you're here, and the first forks)

- **Formal payload:** Definitions a builder must hold precisely — completeness, soundness, **knowledge-soundness** (extractor), zero-knowledge (simulator), succinctness; the SNARK/STARK/argument-vs-proof distinction; the seven layers re-cast as **seven independently-breakable engineering decisions** with a one-line cost-and-failure tag each; the "trust isn't eliminated, it's decomposed" thesis stated as a design principle, not a slogan. - **Sections:** (1) The receipt and what it's worth · (2) Five properties you will trade against each other · (3) The seven decisions, previewed as a tree · (4) How to read this as a builder / auditor / researcher (three paths).

#### Chapter 2 — The First Irreversible Choice: Setup

- **Formal payload:** Trusted vs. transparent setup as a **capex/opex tradeoff**; the **KZG construction shown for real** (commit $g^{p(\tau)}$, open a quotient, verify with **one pairing**) so the ceremony has an anchor; **bilinear pairing mechanics + embedding degree** (why $e(aP,bQ)=e(P,Q)^{ab}$, what makes BN254/BLS12-381 pairing-friendly vs. secp256k1); the **MPC ceremony protocol** (contribute→prove→destroy), **perpetual / updatable Powers of Tau**, **universal vs. circuit-specific SRS**, **random-beacon finalization** and the last-participant attack, **subversion-ZK** (contribution software as an attack vector), **simulation-extractability vs. knowledge-soundness** (Groth16 malleability), SRS degree bound = max circuit size. The **AGM and non-falsifiable assumptions** (Gentry–Wichs: SNARGs can't come from falsifiable assumptions) — *why your "secure" SNARK is only secure in an idealized model.*

#### Chapter 3 — Curves, Fields, and the One-Way Doors
- **Intuition hook:** Choosing a number system is like choosing a foundation for a skyscraper — pick the fast one and you may not be able to put a pairing on the roof later; pick the secure one and every floor costs more. This choice cascades through all six layers above it.
- **Formal payload:** The field/curve decision as the **deepest dependency edge** in the stack. - **Sections:** (1) Big-and-secure vs. small-and-fast · (2) Why this is a one-way door (the cascade) · (3) Curve cycles: the trick that lets proofs verify proofs · (4) The quantum expiration date stamped on half of this.

### PART II — ENCODING THE COMPUTATION (Layers 2–4: getting your program into algebra)

> The front-end build pipeline: language → witness on real hardware → arithmetization. This is where most *bugs* and most *cost* live, so it gets three full chapters and the dominant share of the failure taxonomy.

#### Chapter 4 — Languages and DSLs: What You Write In Decides What You Can Break
- **Intuition hook:** A single character — `=` where `<==` was needed — broke Tornado Cash's soundness. The language you choose decides which bugs are even *possible*, and which the compiler will catch for you before they ship.
- **Formal payload:** The four DSL philosophies as an **engineering decision matrix**: Circom (R1CS, manual constraints, you own correctness), Noir (write-once/prove-anywhere, backend-agnostic), Cairo (ZK-native ISA, language co-designed with the prover), Compact (privacy-by-compilation, disclosure analysis enforced at compile time); plus the lineage that explains them — **ZoKrates** (first high-level DSL), **CirC** (the "compiler, not a language" thesis), Leo, Lurk (recursive/functional). The **front-end = program-to-circuit compiler** abstraction. **Under-constrained circuits** (67% of audited bugs — the dominant failure mode) *and* the neglected **over-constrained / completeness-side bugs**; **refinement types / CODA** and metamorphic testing (MTZK) as compile-time prevention; static analysis (Circomspect).
- **Sections:** (1) Four philosophies, one decision matrix (when to reach for each) · (2) The compiler-protects-you spectrum (Compact's disclosure analysis vs. Circom's "you're on your own") · (3) The under/over-constrained failure class and how tooling kills it.
- **Graph draws:** C94 (Circom, CirC, ZoKrates, DSL-for-ZKP, Constraint Compiler, Circomspect), C3 (Compact, Noir, ZKIR, Disclosure Analysis), C72 (under-constrained), C124 (static analysis), god-node R1CS(91 — as compile target). New: ZoKrates, CirC depth, CODA/refinement types, over-constrained bugs.

#### Chapter 5 — The Witness: The Hidden Bottleneck and the Hardware It Runs On
- **Intuition hook:** Everyone obsesses over proof size. But the slowest, most underestimated part of shipping ZK is the *backstage rehearsal* — generating the witness — and it runs on hardware that can leak your secrets through a stopwatch.
- **Formal payload:** Witness = private execution trace; **witness generation** as a distinct, often-dominant cost that **resists parallelization**; the memory wall (RAM as the binding constraint), the **hardware ladder** (CPU→GPU→FPGA→ASIC); the math that *does* parallelize — **NTT** and **MSM** as the two hot kernels, GPU acceleration (ZKPOG), and why MSM dominates pairing-based provers; **side-channel attacks** as a first-class threat (Zcash Groth16 timing leak, Poseidon cache-timing, EM channel) and **constant-time proving** as an open cost; **witness-constraint divergence** (the witness your code computes vs. what your constraints enforce); non-deterministic hints / guess-and-check; **witness partitioning / continuations** for zkVM parallelism and its correctness hazard (segment boundaries).
- **Sections:** (1) Why witness gen is the bottleneck nobody budgets for · (2) The two kernels that decide your GPU bill (NTT, MSM) · (3) When the walls leak: side channels and constant-time · (4) Streaming, partitioning, and the continuation seam.
- **Graph draws:** C1 (NTT, Witness, Witness Generation, Side-Channel, MSM, Offline Memory Checking, ZKPOG), C19 (Continuations, proving time), C29 (real-time proving). god-nodes Witness Generation, Side-Channel. New depth: hints, witness partitioning, constant-time.

#### Chapter 6 — Arithmetization: Turning Code into Constraints (and Paying the Overhead Tax)
- **Intuition hook:** A laptop evaluates "if balance > threshold" in one nanosecond. A proof can't branch — it only knows multiply and add. That one rule becomes ~50,000 polynomial constraints. The gap between those two numbers is the **overhead tax**, and it's where your dollars go.
- **Formal payload:** The constraint-system menu as an engineering choice: **R1CS** (assembly language, $a\cdot b = c$), **AIR** (state-machine/transition for repetitive traces, STARKs), **PLONKish** (custom gates + copy constraints), unified by **CCS** (the Rosetta Stone — degree parameter, why it generalizes all three). The mechanisms the current book asserts but never builds: **QAP** (R1CS → polynomial divisibility, the algebraic step behind Groth16's 192 bytes — and the BCTV/Pinocchio counterfeiting bug it subverts), the **grand-product / permutation argument** (the accumulator $Z$ that *actually enforces* PLONK copy constraints and Plookup); **lookups** as the modern cost lever (Plookup → LogUp → **LogUp-GKR** → **Lasso** → Jolt's "lookup singularity"); **offline memory checking** (algebraic RAM — a dominant zkVM cost); **sparse/jagged PCS** (commit only occupied rows, what SP1 uses); Randomized AIR (RAPs) and Multilinear AIR. The **three sources of the 10,000×–50,000× tax** and where the floor is.
- **Sections:** (1) Three dialects, one grammar (R1CS/AIR/PLONKish → CCS) · (2) The mechanisms that make them sound (QAP divisibility, grand-product, lookups) · (3) Lookups as the cost revolution · (4) Anatomy of the overhead tax (and its falling floor).
- **Graph draws:** C11 (PLONK, Lookup Argument, Halo2, Arithmetization, AIR, PLONKish, Schwartz-Zippel, Permutation Argument), C37 (CCS, SuperSpartan, multi-folding), C34 (QAP, Selector/Master polynomial, LIP), C23 (Grand Product, Vanishing Poly, HyperPlonk), C15 (Lasso, Jolt), C29 (LogUp-GKR, jagged PCS), C99 (offline memory checking). god-nodes R1CS(91)/PLONK(88)/CCS(49)/Lookup(65). New: QAP construction, grand-product, RAPs/Multilinear AIR, sparse PCS.

### PART III — PROVING (Layers 5–6: the proof core, as engineering choices)

> The "proof core" (Thaler's frame: arithmetization is fused with the proof system and its commitment). Here the necessary theory finally lands at full rigor — but always framed as *the knob that sets your proof size, prover time, verifier cost, and trust model.*

#### Chapter 7 — The Engine Room: IOPs, Sum-Check, and Why Every Modern Prover Looks Alike
- **Intuition hook:** Once you've seen one modern proof system, you've half-seen all of them. PLONK, Marlin, Spartan, HyperNova share a skeleton: **commit to polynomials, then let the verifier interrogate them with random points.** Learn the skeleton once.
- **Formal payload:** The **IOP / Polynomial-IOP model** as the unifying frame (PIOP + PCS = SNARK); the **sum-check protocol** built from scratch (round structure, soundness bound $d/|\mathbb{F}|$, the multilinear variant) — *the 4th-largest god node, the backbone of Spartan/HyperNova/Jolt/SP1*; **multilinear extension (MLE)** and the **boolean hypercube** (a boxed "why multilinear, not univariate" the moment it's load-bearing); **GKR** (layer-by-layer sum-check reduction, prover need not commit the full trace — the LogUp-GKR and verifiable-computation engine); the **Schwartz–Zippel** lemma as the random-evaluation hammer under all of it; the **BCS / Fiat–Shamir transform** (IOP → non-interactive argument via Merkle + hash) and **how to apply FS without breaking soundness** (forward reference to Ch. 14's failure taxonomy). **PCPs / IPs / MIPs** as the lineage, compressed.
- **Sections:** (1) The shared skeleton (PIOP + PCS) · (2) Sum-check from zero · (3) Multilinear, MLE, and GKR · (4) Making it non-interactive (Fiat–Shamir, the BCS transform).

#### Chapter 8 — Choosing a Proving System: The Three Families as Procurement
- **Intuition hook:** Three envelopes on the table. One is tiny but needs a ceremony (Groth16). One is mid-sized and reusable (PLONK). One is fat but needs no trusted setup and survives quantum computers (STARK). You're not picking the "best" — you're picking which costs you can afford to pay.
- **Formal payload:** Groth16 (smallest proof, circuit-specific SRS, pairing-based — *why 192 bytes*, via the QAP from Ch. 6), the **universal-SRS lineage** (Sonic → Marlin → **PLONK**, updatable/universal SRS — the property that makes PLONK-family trust reusable), **STARKs** (transparent, hash-based, post-quantum, FRI), **Spartan** (sum-check + sparse-MLE, no FFT — the transparent multilinear path), **Bulletproofs** (no setup, log-size, linear verify). Decision axes made explicit: **proof size, prover time, verifier time/gas, setup model, PQ-readiness, recursion-friendliness.** A real **selection table** keyed to deployment (rollup vs. coprocessor vs. client-side privacy). The **hybrid STARK-to-SNARK pipeline** (prove fast in a STARK, wrap small in a SNARK for cheap on-chain verification) as the dominant production pattern.
- **Sections:** (1) Three envelopes, six cost axes · (2) The universal-SRS family (Sonic/Marlin/PLONK) and why it won developer mindshare · (3) Transparent & multilinear (STARK, Spartan, Bulletproofs) · (4) The hybrid pipeline everyone actually ships.
- **Graph draws:** C9 (Groth16, STARK, FFLONK, Universal SNARK, FRI), C20 (SNARK, Linear PCP, Pinocchio, GGPR, taxonomy of SNARKs), C28 (Spartan, transparent argument), C8 (Marlin, Supersonic/DARK), C69 (updatable/universal SRS), C24 (STARK-to-SNARK recursion). god-nodes Groth16(116)/PLONK(88)/STARK(83)/SNARK(139). #### Chapter 9 — Polynomial Commitments: The Tradeoff That Sets Everything Downstream
- **Intuition hook:** Underneath every proof system is one component doing the heavy lifting: a way to seal a polynomial in an envelope and later open one point of it. Swap that envelope and you change your proof size, your trust model, and your quantum exposure — *without touching anything above it.*
- **Formal payload:** The **four families as a trilemma-and-its-dissolution**: **KZG** (constant-size, pairing-based, needs SRS), **FRI** (hash-based, transparent, PQ, larger proofs — the Reed-Solomon proximity test, Merkle-committed), **IPA/Bulletproofs** (no setup, log-size, slow verify), **lattice/Ajtai** (PQ, the frontier). Beyond the four: **Dory** (transparent *pairing-based* — disproves "transparent ⇒ no pairings"), **Basefold** (FRI generalized to multilinear over any field), **Hyrax** (multilinear IPA), **Brakedown/Ligero/Orion/Aurora** (linear-time, code-based), **Gemini** (streaming/elastic multilinear-KZG — explains SP1's jagged PCS). The **commit/open/verify cost table** as the master tradeoff sheet; **Merkle trees & vector commitments** built properly here (position-binding, $O(\log n)$ openings) since FRI/STARK rest on them; **Reed–Solomon codes** and proximity/low-degree testing.
- **Sections:** (1) The envelope abstraction and why it's swappable · (2) The four families and the trilemma · (3) Beyond the four (Dory, Basefold, Hyrax, Brakedown) · (4) The cost sheet you'll actually consult.
- **Graph draws:** C8 (PCS god-node, IOP, Marlin, Gemini), C4 (KZG, multilinear KZG), C0 (FRI Commitment), C90 (IPA, Dory, AFGHO/IPP), C74 (Reed-Solomon, Ligero, Orion, Brakedown, Aurora, linear-time code), C23 (BaseFold), C10/C14 (Ajtai, lattice commitments), C33 (Merkle, low-degree testing, proximity test), C6 (vector commitment). god-nodes PCS(123)/KZG(96)/FRI(82). ### PART IV — SCALING (recursion, folding, and the zkVM as the universal product)

> The two levers that turn a proof into infrastructure: compose proofs (recursion) or merge instances cheaply (folding), then package the whole stack as a programmable machine (zkVM).

#### Chapter 10 — Recursion and Aggregation: Proofs That Verify Proofs
- **Intuition hook:** A Russian doll. Put a proof inside a circuit that *verifies* proofs, and prove *that* — now one final proof attests to a million. The catch: the verifier is itself a circuit, and it has to be cheap enough to fit inside another proof.
- **Formal payload:** **Recursive proof composition** (the single highest-signal absent concept — degree 83) built properly; **IVC** formal definition (the two-independence conditions); **PCD** (proof-carrying data — the DAG generalization of IVC's chain); **proof aggregation** vs. recursion (UPA, SHARP, recursion-as-a-service / prover markets); the **cycle-of-curves payoff** from Ch. 3 (verifying a proof over field $\mathbb{F}_q$ inside a circuit over $\mathbb{F}_r$ — why you need MNT/Pasta/BN254-Grumpkin); **STARK-to-SNARK recursion** as compression. The **recursion-vs-folding distinction** stated sharply ("recursion verifies a full SNARK each step; folding defers verification").
- **Sections:** (1) The Russian doll and the verifier-in-a-circuit cost · (2) IVC and PCD (chains and DAGs of computation) · (3) The cycle-of-curves machinery, paid off · (4) Aggregation, prover markets, and recursion as infrastructure.
- **Graph draws:** C24 (Recursive Proof Composition, Proof Aggregation, recursive STARK, STARK-to-SNARK, UPA, SHARP), C18 (IVC, 2-cycle BN254/Grumpkin, SNARK composition, succinct blockchain), C26 (PCD, distributed proof gen, verifiable computation/delegation), C16 (cycles of curves). god-nodes Recursive Proof Composition(83)/IVC(62)/PCD(47). New: recursive-composition standalone, IVC def, PCD, accumulation-vs-recursion.

#### Chapter 11 — Folding: The Snowball That Defers the Hard Work
- **Intuition hook:** Instead of sealing each proof and stacking them (Russian dolls), roll all the instances into one growing snowball and seal it *once* at the end. Folding isn't a SNARK — there's no standalone proof until the final compression. Confusing those two is the most common conceptual error in the field.
- **Formal payload:** **Folding schemes** built from **accumulation** (defer-then-discharge; split vs. atomic accumulation), **reduction of knowledge** (the soundness primitive, finally named); the **Nova genealogy** — Nova (relaxed R1CS) → SuperNova (non-uniform IVC) → HyperNova (CCS, sum-check-based) → ProtoStar/ProtoGalaxy → CycleFold (the practical curve-cycle fix); **Sangria** (PLONKish folding); the **post-quantum frontier**: LatticeFold/LatticeFold+, **Neo** (small-field lattice folding, pay-per-bit), Symphony (production lattice folding); the **bootstrapping↔IVC parallel** (Gentry's "circuit verifies itself"). The **Layer-6 commitment trilemma** as it constrains folding. Concrete: what a folding engine looks like from the inside (pipeline, shared bus, reductions/lanes).
- **Sections:** (1) Snowball vs. Russian doll (folding ≠ recursion ≠ a SNARK) · (2) Accumulation and reduction of knowledge (the mechanism) · (3) The Nova family tree · (4) Post-quantum folding and what a real engine looks like inside.
- **Graph draws:** C5 (R1CS-relaxed, Nova, HyperNova, CycleFold, Halo, ProtoStar, recursion-in-zkVMs), C39 (Folding Scheme, Mangrove, accumulation/split-accumulation, Layer-6 trilemma), C37 (Neo, multi-folding, Symphony), C10 (LatticeFold, Neo), C44 (reduction of knowledge), C31 (split accumulation), C46 (SuperNova). god-nodes Folding Scheme(116)/Nova(68). New: accumulation schemes, reduction of knowledge, Sangria, Neo, bootstrapping↔IVC.

#### Chapter 12 — zkVMs: The Universal Stage You Build On, Not From
- **Intuition hook:** You don't want to hand-write circuits. You want to write a normal program, compile it for a virtual machine, and get a proof for free. The zkVM is the ZK industry's bid to make every developer a ZK developer — and RISC-V quietly won the ISA war.
- **Formal payload:** zkVM architecture as **all seven layers fused into a product**: the ISA choice (**RISC-V** convergence vs. **Cairo**'s ZK-native ISA), the front-end (LLVM/Rust → VM bytecode), **continuations/receipts** (segmenting unbounded execution), the **proof-core triad** (arithmetization + proof system + PCS as one unit). Three live designs through the seven layers: **SP1 Hypercube** (general-purpose, multilinear/hypercube, jagged PCS), **RISC Zero** (RISC-V, continuations, receipts), **Jolt** (the lookup singularity — Lasso-based, "just lookups"), **Cairo/Stwo** (ISA co-designed with the prover, Circle STARKs over M31). **Offline memory checking / RAM circuits** as the dominant cost; **real-time proving** (the 12s-slot threshold for Ethereum enshrined proofs); the **cost collapse** ($80→$0.04/proof). When to reach for a zkVM vs. a hand-rolled circuit (the abstraction-vs-overhead decision).
- **Sections:** (1) Write a program, get a proof (the zkVM promise) · (2) RISC-V vs. ZK-native ISA · (3) Four machines through seven layers (SP1, RISC Zero, Jolt, Cairo/Stwo) · (4) zkVM or hand-rolled circuit? (the decision + cost collapse).
- **Graph draws:** C19 (zkVM, LLVM, RISC Zero, Continuations, Receipt, superoptimization), C0 (SP1 Hypercube, Circle STARKs, M31, Cairo, RISC Zero, Stwo, Airbender), C29 (zkEVM, real-time proving, LogUp-GKR, Jagged PCS, multilinear), C15 (Jolt, Lasso), C92 (verifiable state machine, RAM verification, Von Neumann, circuit generator), C99 (offline memory checking). god-nodes zkVM(59)/SP1(47). ### PART V — SHIPPING (Layer 7: on-chain, secure, in production)

- **Formal payload:** The systematic **Chaliasos SoK 5-class taxonomy** (under-constrained, over-constrained/completeness, computation-constraint mismatch, …); the **two-class Fiat–Shamir failure taxonomy** — transcript-incompleteness (**Frozen Heart**) vs. adaptive **correlation-intractability** attacks (the Last-Challenge attack, Solana ZK-ElGamal) — *which is why the ROM is needed*; **soundness attacks** (accepting proof for a false statement in an FS-compiled argument); **governance as the Achilles heel** (Beanstalk $182M flash-loan governance attack, Tornado Cash governance attack) — the seventh layer is a committee; **subversion / SRS attacks** (callback to Ch. 2); the **"who verifies the verifier"** problem; **formal verification** as the emerging defense. The unifying lesson: *each of the seven layers has its own breach class; security is a per-layer audit.*
- **Sections:** (1) The five circuit-bug classes (under/over-constrained and friends) · (2) When the transcript lies (the two-class FS taxonomy) · (3) Governance and the social layer · (4) The defense stack (formal verification, audits, who verifies the verifier).

## 3. Reading-order rationale

The order is the **build pipeline**, which is also a clean prerequisite chain:

1. **Part I (Decide)** installs vocabulary and forces the two *earliest, most irreversible* decisions — setup and curve/field — before anything depends on them. Setup (Ch. 2) and curve choice (Ch. 3) are first because, as the manuscript's own dependency note says, the choice of primitive *cascades upward through every layer*; a builder must internalize that cascade before walking the layers. Pairings and KZG are introduced here (not in a late "primitives" chapter) because the SRS ceremony is meaningless without them — *intuition (trapdoor) → formalism (one pairing check)* in the same chapter.

2. **Part II (Encode)** walks the front-end in literal build order: language → witness → arithmetization. This is deliberately front-loaded because the empirical reality (67% of bugs at the circuit layer; witness gen as the hidden bottleneck) is that *most practitioner pain lives here*, not in the proof system.

3. **Part III (Prove)** is the theory core, placed exactly when the reader has a concrete artifact (a constraint system from Ch. 6) that needs proving. Sum-check/IOP/GKR/MLE land here because *now they're load-bearing*: the reader meets MLE the moment they must choose a commitment scheme, satisfying the Feynman "use it, then formalize it" spiral. Commitments (Ch. 9) come last in the part because they're the swappable component every proof system above depends on.

4. **Part IV (Scale)** follows because recursion/folding/zkVMs *consume* everything in Parts I–III — recursion needs the cycle-of-curves fact from Ch. 3 and the proof systems from Ch. 8; folding needs accumulation and the commitment trilemma from Ch. 9; the zkVM fuses all seven layers, so it can only be understood after each layer is individually known.

5. **Part V (Ship)** is the on-chain/security/PQ payoff — the verifier, the breach taxonomy, the migration — placed last because every breach class and every gas cost references a specific layer the reader has now built.

6. **Part VI (Deployments)** is the synthesis + application wing: one fully-worked system (Midnight), the application catalog, the PET context, the thesis DAG, and the frontier. It comes last because the causal-DAG thesis (Ch. 19) is only convincing after all seven decisions are concrete, and the application map (Ch. 17) is only actionable once the reader can match a proof system to each use case.

The **spiral** is explicit: pairings appear as intuition in Ch. 2, are used in Ch. 8 (Groth16), and are formalized in Ch. 9 (KZG cost sheet); sum-check appears as a name in Ch. 6 (lookups/LogUp-GKR), is built in Ch. 7, and is the engine of folding in Ch. 11; the cycle-of-curves is a curve fact in Ch. 3 and a recursion payoff in Ch. 10.

## 5. Risks / tradeoffs of this structure

1. **Pulling "Layer 6" (curves/fields) forward into Part I (Ch. 3) breaks the strict layer numbering.** This is the deliberate central bet of the systems lens: the manuscript itself admits dependencies run *downward* (primitive → proof system → arithmetization → language → setup), so a builder must confront the curve/field one-way door before walking the stack. *Risk:* a reader expecting a clean Layer-1…7 march is briefly disoriented. *Mitigation:* Ch. 1's "seven decisions" preview explicitly states that the build order differs from the numeric order, and each chapter is tagged with its layer.

2. **The theory core (Part III) is dense and could stall a pure practitioner.** Sum-check, IOPs, GKR, and MLE are real mathematics. *Risk:* the builder who only wants to ship may bounce off Ch. 7. *Mitigation:* the Feynman spiral (each concept is *used* in Ch. 6 before it's *built* in Ch. 7) and a hard rule that every section opens with the decision it unlocks; Ch. 7 is explicitly flagged in "how to read this" as skimmable-on-first-pass for the pure shipping path.

3. **22 chapters is long, and Parts V–VI risk overlap.** Splitting Layer 7 into three chapters (13/14/15) and adding an application chapter (17) and PET chapter (18) creates seams where vulnerability/economics/deployment material could be double-covered. *Risk:* redundancy, page bloat. *Mitigation:* strict ownership — Ch. 13 owns *cost/economics*, Ch. 14 owns *failure*, Ch. 17 owns *use cases*; Part VI could collapse to 3 chapters (merge 18 into 17, merge 20 into 19) if length forces it.

4. **Front-loading witness/hardware (Ch. 5) before the proof system is unconventional.** Most books teach the proof system before the witness machinery. *Risk:* the reader meets NTT/MSM/side-channels before they fully know what's being proved. *Mitigation:* Ch. 1 + Ch. 6's overhead-tax framing give enough scaffolding; the payoff is that the *real* bottleneck (witness gen) gets the prominence its degree-and-cost reality demands, which is exactly the systems lens's point.

5. **The application catalog (Ch. 17) spans wildly different maturity levels** (production rollups next to research ZKML/media-provenance). *Risk:* dating quickly, or implying parity between shipped and speculative tech. *Mitigation:* every entry carries an explicit maturity tag (production/growth/pilot/research) and a proof-system match, so the chapter reads as a decision map rather than a hype list.

6. **Decision-orientation can crowd out "why it's true."** A builder-first frame risks under-serving the reader who wants the security *proof*, not just the cost table. *Mitigation:* Part III is the deliberate counterweight — it locks down soundness bounds and the AGM honestly — and Ch. 14 makes the failure modes rigorous, so the book still satisfies the auditor/researcher paths the manuscript's "how to read" section promises.
